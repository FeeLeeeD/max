# A/B Testing Email Campaigns

A/B testing, sometimes called split testing, is the practice of sending two variants of the same campaign to comparable audience samples and comparing their performance on a predefined success metric. For email, the typical mechanics are straightforward: hold out a small randomized slice of the list, split it between variant A and variant B, wait long enough to collect statistically meaningful data, and then either send the winning variant to the remainder of the list or roll the learning into the next campaign. Disciplined teams treat every send as an opportunity to learn something, not just as a one-shot delivery.

The single most important rule is to test one variable at a time. Changing subject line and the hero image in the same experiment makes it impossible to attribute any observed lift to a specific cause. Common single-variable tests include subject line phrasing, preheader text, sender name, send time, call-to-action copy, button color, hero image choice, and overall message length. Always write down the hypothesis before launching — for example, "a curiosity-driven subject line will outperform a benefit-driven one for our engaged segment" — and commit to the success metric in advance so you cannot rationalize a loss after the fact.

Sample size is where most email A/B tests quietly fall apart. Tiny lists produce noisy results that look dramatic but do not replicate; a three-point difference on a sample of two hundred recipients is almost certainly random noise. A rough rule of thumb is that detecting a 10% relative lift in open rate at typical marketing baselines requires several thousand recipients per variant. When your list is smaller than that, pool results across several consecutive campaigns rather than making decisions from a single noisy test.

Finally, resist the temptation to call a winner before the test is complete. Early results are volatile, and peeking at the data repeatedly effectively inflates your false-positive rate. Pick your run length in advance, let the experiment finish, record the outcome in a shared document regardless of whether it confirmed your hypothesis, and use the accumulating body of evidence to guide the next round of tests.
