# Click-Through Rate

Click-through rate, commonly abbreviated CTR, measures the percentage of delivered emails in which the recipient clicked at least one tracked link. The standard formula divides unique clicks by delivered messages and multiplies the result by one hundred. Because every click represents an intentional action, CTR is widely considered the most reliable per-message indicator of whether the content and offer inside the email actually resonated with the reader.

Benchmarks for CTR depend heavily on the type of campaign. Broadcast marketing emails typically land between 2% and 5%, while triggered lifecycle messages such as password resets, order confirmations, and re-engagement flows routinely exceed 10% because they arrive at moments of high user intent. Newsletters sit somewhere in between, with engagement driven as much by editorial quality as by send cadence. Comparing against your own historical baseline is always more meaningful than comparing against an external industry average.

Several levers consistently move CTR. Clear, action-oriented copy near the top of the message usually outperforms long preambles, especially on mobile where the first viewport is all most readers ever see. A single primary call-to-action beats a dense list of links because it removes the decision cost for the reader. Personalization beyond the first name helps when it reflects real behavior, such as referencing a specific product category the subscriber has purchased or browsed. Segmentation has the largest compound effect of all, because sending a genuinely relevant message to a smaller audience almost always beats sending a generic message to the full list.

Some teams prefer click-to-open rate, which divides unique clicks by unique opens rather than by delivered messages. This isolates the quality of the email body from the performance of the subject line, which is useful when diagnosing whether a weak campaign suffered from poor attention capture at the inbox level or from weak content once opened.
